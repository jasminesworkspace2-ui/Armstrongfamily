The Story of This Family · ca. 1780s to Present

Bondage to Belonging

How one family survived slavery, claimed land, built lives, and made it to California

Prologue

This is the story of a family that was not supposed to survive — not intact, not named, not remembered. The system that held them was designed to prevent exactly this: a great-great-great-granddaughter sitting down with documents and DNA and piecing together the names of women who lived two hundred years ago in South Carolina.

But here we are. The names survived. The family survived. And because Jasmine M. Lee asked the questions, we know who they were.

Chapter One · South Carolina · ca. 1780s–1839
Patience, and What Was Done to Her

Sometime in the 1780s, a woman named Patience was born — likely in South Carolina, possibly in Union County. She was enslaved her entire life. On August 4, 1795, Francis Davenport Sr. of Newberry County signed a deed giving Patience to his daughter Martha, called Patty, who had married Hezekiah Waldrop. The deed said Patience was given "with her increase" — a legal phrase meaning all children she might ever have would automatically belong to the Waldrop family. She had no say. Her future children had no say.

Patience had children. One of them was named Clarissa, born approximately 1807. The 1839 Waldrop estate deed names Patience's children: Clarissa, Rachel, Tom, William, Amanda, and Harriet. Six people. All enslaved. All distributed among white heirs like furniture.

"one Negro girl named Patience with her increase" — Newberry County SC Deed Book C:367, August 4, 1795

The Waldrop family moved — South Carolina to Georgia to Tennessee to Mississippi. Patience and her children moved with them, not by choice. Hezekiah's daughter Fanny eventually married a man named John Nichols and moved to Texas, bringing enslaved people with her. This is how Patience's descendants came to Bellville, Texas.

Chapter Two · Texas · 1834–1865
Piney Creek, Bellville, Austin County

John Nichols arrived in Austin County, Texas in 1834. He was granted 4,428 acres of land along Piney Creek — the J. Nichols Survey, Abstract No. 73. On his plantation, enslaved people worked the land and raised children who were also enslaved. In 1860, the U.S. Census recorded nine enslaved people in his household — listed by age and gender only, with no names given.

Among them: a female age 43 born approximately 1817 in North Carolina, and a female age 13 born approximately 1848 in Texas. The female age 43 was almost certainly Rendy Blayn — Easter Bell's mother. The female age 13 was almost certainly Easter Bell herself — born July 4, 1848, on the Nichols plantation on Piney Creek. Easter Bell was born on Independence Day. She would wait seventeen years for her own.

She was born on July 4, 1848 — Independence Day — on land that bore the name of the man who owned her. She stayed on that land for the rest of her life. After 1865, it was hers too.
Chapter Three · 1865 and After
What Freedom Looked Like in Bellville, Texas

When emancipation came in June 1865, Easter Bell was seventeen years old. She stayed in Bellville. On December 18, 1865, she married Joseph Nichols — also formerly enslaved on the Nichols plantation, who had taken the surname of his enslaver. Together they built a family. They had twelve children over the next thirty years.

At some point after emancipation, Easter Bell's family acquired two acres of land — a small portion of the same J. Nichols Survey where she had been born enslaved. A formerly enslaved woman, on land that bore her enslaver's name, held by her family for the next 141 years.

Chapter Four · Fort Worth · 1900–1953
Eva Armstrong Ballard Moves North

Easter Bell and Joseph's daughter Hellen Nichols married John Armstrong — whose father David had also been enslaved in Upshur County, Texas. Their daughter Eva Armstrong was born on June 27, 1900 in Bellville. John Armstrong was killed in Fort Worth in 1911. Eva was eleven years old.

Eva grew up in Fort Worth, married James Wilson Ballard Sr., ran a Night Club and Cafe in San Antonio, owned property, raised her children, and eventually made it to California — to Garden Grove, Orange County — where she died on November 24, 1977. Three generations from slavery to the Pacific coast.

Chapter Five · Santa Ana, California · 1929–1988
Margie Nell Ballard Corbin

Margie Nell Ballard was born in Fort Worth, Texas on March 13, 1929. She was Eva's daughter, Hellen's granddaughter, Easter Bell's great-granddaughter. She settled in Santa Ana, California. She raised seven children on Townsend Street. She died on October 15, 1988 in Orange County.

Her daughter Hjordis L. Corbin was born January 20, 1960 in Los Angeles. Hjordis's daughter Jasmine M. Lee was born January 25, 1982 in Orange County, California.

Epilogue · March 2026
What This Research Found

Patience existed. Clarissa existed. Easter Bell existed. Hellen existed. Eva existed. Margie existed. They were real women with real names and real lives. The system that held them tried to make them invisible. It did not succeed.

From Patience in Newberry County, South Carolina in 1795 to Jasmine M. Lee in Orange County, California in 2026 — that is the distance this family has traveled. That is what belonging looks like, built out of bondage, one generation at a time.

Patience is the earliest ancestor we have confirmed through primary source documents. She was a real person. Her children were real people. This family existed before the records tried to erase them. — Jasmine M. Lee, March 2026

The Enslaving Families · For Research Context Only

Nichols · Waldrop

How Two Slaveholding Families Were Connected — and Why It Matters for This Research

The families documented here are not blood relatives. They are the enslaving families whose records — deeds, slave schedules, land grants, wills — are the only paper trail that documents our ancestors' existence. Understanding these families is essential to tracing the history of Patience, Clarissa, Joseph Nichols, and Easter Bell.

The Waldrop Family
Hezekiah Waldrop and the Davenport Connection

Hezekiah Parrish Waldrop was born in 1772 in Newberry County, South Carolina. His father was John Waldrop, whose will names seven sons including Zekiah (Hezekiah). Hezekiah married Martha "Patty" Davenport — the daughter of Francis Davenport Sr. This marriage is the critical connection: two of Davenport's daughters married Waldrop men. When Francis Davenport distributed enslaved people to his daughters in 1795, he gave Patience to Patty Waldrop on August 4, 1795.

The Waldrop family migrated from South Carolina through Georgia, Tennessee, and Mississippi, bringing enslaved people with them at every stage. Hezekiah's 1839 estate deed in Rankin County, Mississippi confirmed Patience and her children were distributed among his heirs.

The Nichols Family
John Nichols and the Austin County Plantation

John Nichols arrived in Austin County, Texas in 1834 as one of Stephen F. Austin's original colonists. He was granted 4,428 acres along Piney Creek — the J. Nichols Survey, Abstract No. 73. He married Frances Jane Waldroup — confirmed by DNA match Sherry Newman Welty as Fanny Waldrop, daughter of Hezekiah Waldrop. This is the critical link: the enslaved people from the Waldrop household came to the Nichols plantation through this marriage.

The Critical Connection

Fanny Waldrop — Hezekiah's daughter — married John Nichols and brought enslaved people from her father's household into the Nichols plantation. This means the same family network — Davenport, Waldrop, Nichols — held your ancestors in bondage across two states and eight decades. Patience was enslaved by the Waldrop family in South Carolina. Her descendants came to Texas through Fanny Waldrop's marriage to John Nichols. After emancipation, Easter Bell's family stayed on the J. Nichols Survey land.

"Fanny married third to John Nichols who all lived in Bellville, Austin, Texas, where I grew up. I always suspected that there are Africans in my family and I'm glad to finally know of you!" — Sherry Newman Welty, DNA match, March 2026

Property Research · Three Properties · Three Stories

The Land

How This Family Acquired Land, What Happened to It, and What Was Found

Three properties have been identified in this family's history. Two were lost to tax sales. One was legally settled by all heirs. All three tell the story of a family that fought to own something — again and again — in the century after emancipation.

Property One · Lost 2006
The Bellville Land
2 Acres · J. Nichols Survey · Abstract No. 73 · Account R20014 · Bellville, Austin County, Texas
Lost — Tax Sale 2006 — Legal Review Possible
The Story

Sometime after emancipation in 1865, Easter Bell Nichols's family acquired two acres of land in the J. Nichols Survey in Bellville — the same survey that bore the name of the man who had enslaved them. They stayed on that land for 141 years.

The land passed through multiple generations. In 1997, Austin County filed a tax suit — Cause No. 97V-003 — naming all heirs as defendants. Most were already dead. Easter Bell herself, who died in 1935, was named as Easter Pink. Judgment was rendered December 14, 1999. On August 1, 2006, a Sheriff's Deed conveyed the two acres to Austin County for the use of Bellville Independent School District. The land was sold for back taxes.

Property Two · Lost 2017 · Most Actionable
The Waco Land
Lots 4–5–6 · Block 3 · Lincoln Park Addition · 724 East Johnson Street · Waco, McLennan County, Texas
Lost — Tax Sale 2017 — Contact Legal Aid First
The Story

Rev. George W. Ballard — Eva Armstrong Ballard's father-in-law — owned property at 724 East Johnson Street in Waco, Texas, recorded in McLennan County Deed Records, Volume 289, Page 655. On November 7, 2017, the property was sold in a tax sale to McQuades for $1,600 — 80 years after Rev. Ballard died in Waco on October 24, 1937. The 2017 sale is the most recent and most actionable for potential legal recovery.

Property Three · Sold Legally 1873
Newton Bullard's 160 Acres
Survey No. 124 · Caney Creek · Upshur County, Texas · 160 acres
Legal Sale — No Recourse — Fully Documented
The Story

Newton Bullard — formerly enslaved, grandfather of James Wilson Ballard Sr. — claimed 160 acres on Survey No. 124 along Caney Creek in Upshur County, Texas. Patent filed August 26, 1856 — Texas GLO File No. 3378, Patent No. 595, Volume 46. On August 9, 1873, Newton Bullard sold the 160 acres to E.G. Horn for $250 in gold. This was a legal sale. This land cannot be legally recovered.

Newton's grandson James Wilson Ballard Sr. was born on August 26, 1897 — the same calendar date as the 1856 land patent. The land and the man share a birthdate across generations.
