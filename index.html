Your 3rd Great-Grandmother · July 4, 1848 – December 17, 1935 Easter Bell The Woman This Entire Story Is About

If there is one person at the center of this family's history, it is Easter Bell Nichols. She was born enslaved on July 4, 1848, in Bellville, Texas. She died free on December 17, 1935, in the same town, at 87 years old. Between those two dates she raised twelve children, held onto two acres of land for the rest of her life, and watched her grandchildren begin building lives across Texas and eventually California. She did all of this on land that bore the name of the man who had once owned her.

She did not leave. She did not need to. The land was hers now.

Her death certificate lists her mother as Rendy Blayn. That single line connects two generations — and it is one of the most important pieces of information in this entire research project.
Born Into Bondage · July 4, 1848 Independence Day, Piney Creek, Bellville, Texas

Easter Bell was born on July 4, 1848 — Independence Day — on the John Nichols plantation along Piney Creek in Bellville, Texas. She was born enslaved. Her mother was Rendy Blayn, a woman born in North Carolina around 1817. Mother and daughter appear together in the 1860 slave schedule: a female age 43 and a female age 13 — no names, just numbers, because that was all the law required.

Her father is listed as unknown on her death certificate. That silence in the record says a great deal on its own — it was a common and painful reality for enslaved women, whose children often had unknown or unacknowledged fathers.

Easter Bell spent the first seventeen years of her life on that plantation. In 1865, everything changed.

Freedom · 1865 · What She Did With It Twelve Children and 141 Years of Land

On December 18, 1865 — six months after emancipation — Easter Bell married Joseph Nichols. Over the next thirty years they had twelve children. At some point after emancipation the family acquired two acres of land in the J. Nichols Survey — the same survey named for her former enslaver. Her family held that land for 141 years.

Later in life, after Joseph died between 1900 and 1910, Easter Bell married Hannibal Pink on July 30, 1914. By 1920 she was listed as widowed and head of household. She kept going. She always kept going.

She died on December 17, 1935 in Bellville. She was buried there. She had lived through slavery, the Civil War, emancipation, Reconstruction, Jim Crow, and into the Great Depression. She was 87 years old. She had outlived her first husband, her daughter Hellen, and her daughter Katie. And she had held onto her land the whole time.

Eva Armstrong Ballard · 1900–1977

Eva Armstrong Ballard is the person your mother and her siblings actually remember. Not through documents — through stories. The way she carried herself. The business she ran. The way she held her family together across cities and decades and hardships that would have broken most people. She is the living memory anchor for the generation that knew her, and she is the reason this family made it to California.

She was born on June 27, 1900 in Bellville, Texas. She died on November 24, 1977 in Garden Grove, California. In between she traveled the full length of this family's story — from the plantation land of Austin County to the Pacific coast — and she did it on her own terms.

Bellville to Fort Worth · 1900–1911 The Daughter of Hellen Nichols Armstrong

Eva was born in Bellville — the same town where her grandmother Easter Bell had been born enslaved in 1848. Her mother was Hellen Nichols Armstrong, Easter Bell's daughter. Her father was John Armstrong, whose own father David had been enslaved in Upshur County, Texas.

Eva was eleven years old when her father was killed. On August 30, 1911, John Armstrong was shot by a man named Jack Thompson in Fort Worth. Thompson was later acquitted. John Armstrong was 39 years old. Eva grew up after that without her father — in a household run by her mother Hellen, who worked as a waitress and kept her children together on her own until she died in 1931.

That kind of resilience — keeping yourself and your children going after loss — is something Eva learned early. She watched her mother do it. She would do it herself, and do it bigger.

Fort Worth to San Antonio · Marriage and Business Eva Armstrong Runs a Night Club and Cafe

Eva married James Wilson Ballard Sr. — the son of Reverend George W. Ballard, whose own grandfather Newton Bullard had claimed 160 acres of Texas land in 1856 as a formerly enslaved person. James and Eva had six children: James Jr., Margie Nell, Lois, Selma (who died in infancy), Betty, and Charles Ray.

In San Antonio, Eva ran a Night Club and Cafe. She owned property. She was a businesswoman in a city and an era that did not make things easy for Black women who wanted to own things and run things. She did it anyway.

On April 22, 1944, Eva purchased Lot 13, Block 2, NCB 2924 in San Antonio — in her own name, jointly with James Sr. and James Jr. She paid $3,600. In 1957, after James Sr. had died, all five surviving children signed the property over to Eva. She then sold it to Emmie Garrett on January 22, 1957. She knew how to close a transaction. She knew how to protect what she had built.

She ran a Night Club and Cafe in San Antonio. She owned property. She knew how to close a transaction. That is not luck. That is character.
California · The Final Chapter Garden Grove, Orange County · 1977

Eva eventually married again — to Minor Green — and made it to California. She died on November 24, 1977 in Garden Grove, Orange County, as Eva Green. She was 77 years old.

Your mother and her siblings remember Eva. That memory is a primary source of its own kind — more vivid than any census record or property deed. Whatever they remember about her is true in the way that only living memory can be true.

If you knew Eva Armstrong — if your mother or your aunts or uncles ever told you a story about her — those stories belong in this archive too. This page will grow. If you have a memory of Eva, a photograph, or a story passed down, contact Jasmine so it can be added here.

The Story of This Family · ca. 1780s to Present Bondage to Belonging How one family survived slavery, claimed land, built lives, and made it to California

This is the story of a family that was not supposed to survive — not intact, not named, not remembered. The system that held them was designed to prevent exactly this: a great-great-great-granddaughter sitting down with documents and DNA and piecing together the names of women who lived two hundred years ago in South Carolina. But here we are. The names survived. The family survived.

Chapter One · South Carolina · ca. 1780s–1839 Patience, and What Was Done to Her

Sometime in the 1780s, a woman named Patience was born in South Carolina. On August 4, 1795, Francis Davenport Sr. signed a deed giving Patience to his daughter Patty, who had married Hezekiah Waldrop. The deed said Patience was given "with her increase" — meaning all children she might ever have would automatically belong to the Waldrop family by law.

Patience had children. One of them was named Clarissa, born approximately 1807. The 1839 Waldrop estate deed names Patience's children: Clarissa, Rachel, Tom, William, Amanda, and Harriet. Six people. All enslaved. All distributed among white heirs like furniture.

"one Negro girl named Patience with her increase" — Newberry County SC Deed Book C:367, August 4, 1795
Chapter Two · Texas · 1834–1865 Piney Creek, Bellville, Austin County

John Nichols arrived in Austin County, Texas in 1834. His daughter-in-law Fanny Waldrop — Hezekiah's daughter — married John Nichols, bringing enslaved people from the Waldrop household into the Nichols plantation. In 1860, nine enslaved people lived on that plantation. Among them: Rendy Blayn, a woman from North Carolina, and her daughter Easter Bell — born July 4, 1848, on Piney Creek. Easter Bell was born on Independence Day. She would wait seventeen years for her own.

Chapter Three · 1865 and After What Freedom Looked Like in Bellville, Texas

When emancipation came in June 1865, Easter Bell was seventeen years old. She stayed in Bellville. On December 18, 1865, she married Joseph Nichols. They had twelve children. The family acquired two acres of land in the J. Nichols Survey — on land still bearing her enslaver's name. 141 years that land stayed in the family.

Chapter Four · Fort Worth · 1900–1953 Eva Armstrong Ballard Moves North

Easter Bell's granddaughter Eva Armstrong was born June 27, 1900 in Bellville. Her father was killed when she was eleven. She grew up in Fort Worth, ran a Night Club and Cafe in San Antonio, owned property, and eventually died in Garden Grove, California in 1977. Three generations from slavery to the Pacific coast.

Chapter Five · Santa Ana, California · 1929–Present Margie Nell Ballard Corbin — and Her Children

Margie Nell Ballard was born in Fort Worth on March 13, 1929. She settled in Santa Ana, California, raised seven children on Townsend Street, and died October 15, 1988 in Orange County. Her daughter Hjordis gave birth to Jasmine M. Lee on January 25, 1982 — the researcher who found all of this and brought it back.

From Patience in Newberry County, South Carolina in 1795 to Jasmine M. Lee in Orange County, California in 2026 — that is the distance this family has traveled. That is what belonging looks like, built out of bondage, one generation at a time.

Confirmed · Probable · Possible · Research Status Noted The Enslaved Family What Each Person Endured, What Was Confirmed, What Families Were Separated

Every person documented here was held as property against their will. They could not legally marry, own land, keep their children, or control where they lived. This page names them, documents what we know about what happened to them, and is honest about what the records confirm versus what is probable but not yet proven.

How to Read the Status Labels

Confirmed — verified by a primary source document (deed, slave schedule, census, death certificate).
Probable — strong evidence exists but a primary source confirmation is pending.
Possible — a credible lead that has not yet been confirmed by a document.

● Confirmed — Primary Source: 1795 SC Deed Book C:367 · 1839 MS Waldrop Estate Deed Patience ca. 1780s — unknown · Newberry County, South Carolina → Mississippi

Patience was given away as property by Francis Davenport Sr. to his daughter Patty Waldrop on August 4, 1795. The deed included "with her increase" — meaning all of her children were automatically enslaved by the Waldrop family by law, with no additional transaction needed.

What she endured: Patience was transferred between slaveholding families at least once — from the Davenport household to the Waldrop household. She was enslaved for her entire life. Her children were distributed among white heirs through her enslaver's 1839 estate deed.

Family separation: In 1839, Hezekiah Waldrop's estate distributed Patience's children — Clarissa, Rachel, Tom, William, Amanda, and Harriet — among his heirs. Whether this separated them from Patience or from each other is not yet documented.

Rendy Blayn is Easter Bell's mother, confirmed by Easter Bell's 1935 death certificate — Field 15: Mother's Maiden Name: Rendy Blayn. She was born in North Carolina around 1817 and was brought to Texas, likely as an enslaved person under the Nichols household.

What she endured: Rendy was enslaved in North Carolina and transported to Texas — a forced migration away from whatever family and community she had. Family separation: Rendy was separated from whatever family she had in North Carolina when she was brought to Texas. Her fate after emancipation in 1865 has not yet been documented. She does not appear in post-war census records under a confirmed name.

What she endured: Easter Bell was born into slavery, lived on the same plantation her entire childhood, and had no legal rights until she was 17. The enslaved people documented here survived. Their names reached us. June 19, 1865 · Galveston, Texas · Our Family Was There Juneteenth How the Day America Honors Changed Our Family's Life Forever
June 19, 1865 General Order No. 3 · Galveston, Texas

On June 19, 1865, Union soldiers arrived in Galveston, Texas and read General Order No. 3, announcing that all enslaved people in Texas were free. This was two and a half years after the Emancipation Proclamation had been signed. The delay was not an accident — Texas was one of the last Confederate states to receive the news, and enslavers had suppressed and withheld the information as long as they could.

That day is now celebrated as Juneteenth — a federal holiday, a day of remembrance, and a day of joy. And it connects directly to this family.

Our Family's Connection Easter Bell Was Seventeen Years Old

When General Order No. 3 was read in Galveston on June 19, 1865, Easter Bell Nichols was seventeen years old. She was living on the John Nichols plantation on Piney Creek in Bellville, Austin County, Texas — approximately 150 miles from Galveston.

Easter Bell had been born enslaved on that plantation on July 4, 1848. She had spent her entire life there. When the announcement reached Bellville in June 1865, she was free for the first time in her life. Six months later — on December 18, 1865 — she married Joseph Nichols.

Juneteenth is not an abstract holiday to this family. It is the specific moment when Easter Bell — your 3rd great-grandmother, the woman at the center of this entire family history — became legally free. Everything that followed in this family's story flows from that date.

Also in Texas on Juneteenth 1865 Other Family Members Who Were Freed That Day

Rendy Blayn, Easter Bell's mother — born in North Carolina, brought to Texas against her will, enslaved on Piney Creek her entire adult life — was also freed on June 19, 1865. She was approximately 48 years old. What happened to her after emancipation is still being researched.

Joseph Nichols, who would become Easter Bell's husband six months later — also enslaved on the Nichols plantation — was freed that same day. He was approximately 18 years old. Within six months he had married and begun building a family.

David Armstrong, Easter Bell's future son-in-law's father — enslaved in Upshur County, Texas — was also freed in June 1865. His son John Armstrong was born in Bellville in 1872, married Hellen Nichols, and was Eva Armstrong's father.

Newton Bullard, Eva's husband's grandfather — freed in Texas in 1865 — went on to claim 160 acres of land. He had already filed his land patent in 1856, suggesting he had found some way to stake that claim even before emancipation. His exact circumstances before June 19, 1865 are still being researched.

Juneteenth in the Family Today What This Holiday Means for Our Line

Juneteenth became a federal holiday on June 17, 2021. For many American families, it is a day to celebrate a historical event. For this family, it is the specific anniversary of the moment when Easter Bell Nichols — the woman this entire archive is about — became free.

Every Juneteenth, this family is not celebrating an abstraction. They are celebrating Easter Bell. They are celebrating Rendy Blayn. They are celebrating Joseph Nichols. They are celebrating the specific day when the long chain of bondage that began with Patience in 1795 South Carolina was finally broken — at least legally — in Bellville, Texas in 1865.

Everything in this archive that comes after June 19, 1865 — the twelve children, the two acres, Eva's Night Club and Cafe, Margie's family in Santa Ana, Jasmine's research in 2026 — all of it begins on that day.

The Enslaving Families · Research Context Only Nichols · Waldrop How Two Slaveholding Families Were Connected — and Why It Matters

These families are not blood relatives. They are the enslaving families whose documents — deeds, slave schedules, land grants, wills — are the only paper trail that confirms our ancestors existed. Understanding how these families were connected is essential to tracing Easter Bell, Rendy Blayn, Clarissa, and Patience.

The Waldrop Family Hezekiah Waldrop and the Davenport Connection

Hezekiah Waldrop (1772–ca.1840) married Martha Patty Davenport, daughter of Francis Davenport Sr. of Newberry County, SC. This marriage is the critical connection: when Davenport distributed enslaved people to his daughters in 1795, he gave Patience to Patty Waldrop on August 4, 1795. The Waldrop family migrated from SC through GA, TN, and into MS, bringing enslaved people at every stage. Hezekiah's 1839 estate deed distributed Patience's children among his heirs.

The Nichols Family John Nichols and the Austin County Plantation

John Nichols arrived in Austin County, Texas in 1834, granted 4,428 acres along Piney Creek — the J. Nichols Survey, Abstract No. 73. He married Frances Jane Waldroup — confirmed by DNA match Sherry Newman Welty (March 2026) as Fanny Waldrop, Hezekiah's daughter. This is the critical link: enslaved people from the Waldrop household came to the Nichols plantation through this marriage. In 1860, nine enslaved people lived there — including Rendy Blayn and her daughter Easter Bell.

The Waldrop family migrated here from South Carolina through Georgia and Tennessee, bringing Patience and her children. For Family Members Who Have Never Done a DNA Test Understanding DNA What the Numbers Mean · How We Found Family · Why DNA Confirmed What Documents Couldn't

DNA testing has been one of the most powerful tools in this research. But the numbers and terms can be confusing if you haven't encountered them before. This page explains everything in plain language — no science degree required.

What Is a DNA Test? The Basics

A DNA test for genealogy (ancestry tracing) works by analyzing your genetic material — usually from a saliva sample — and comparing it to thousands of other people who have tested. When you share a significant amount of DNA with someone, the testing company flags it as a match. The more DNA you share, the more closely related you are.

The most important number in DNA genealogy is called the centiMorgan (cM). Think of it as a unit of genetic similarity. The higher the number, the more closely related two people are.

What Do the cM Numbers Mean?
3,400+ cMParent or child — you share about half your DNA
1,700–2,300 cMGrandparent/grandchild, sibling, aunt/uncle, or half-sibling
700–1,200 cMFirst cousin, great-grandparent, great-aunt/uncle
200–700 cMFirst cousin once or twice removed, half first cousin
50–200 cMSecond cousin, second cousin once removed
Below 50 cMThird cousin or more distant — still related but very far back
